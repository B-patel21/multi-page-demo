The Giant Panda

Habitat

Liveing mainly in temperate forests high in the mountains of southwest China lives one of the world's rarest mammals: the giant panda, also called the panda.

Diet

Pandas eat almost nothing but bamboo shoots and leaves. Occasionally they eat other vegetation, fish, or small mammals, but bamboo accounts for 99 percent of their diets.Pandas eat fast, they eat a lot, and they spend about 12 hours a day eating.The reason is that they digest only about a fifth of what they eat. Overall, bamboo is not very nutritious. To stay healthy, they have to eat a lot—up to 15 percent of their body weight in 12 hours—so they eat fast.

Panda Features

The shape of the teeth helps the animals crush the bamboo shoots, leaves, and stems that they eat. They can chomp on bamboo up to one-and-a-half inches thick. To get the bamboo to their mouths, they hold the stems with their front paws, which have enlarged wrist bones that act as thumbs for gripping. An adult female panda weighs 200 pounds. Pandas can climb as high as 13,000 feet and are also very good swimmers. Sometimes male pandas relax by doing handstands against trees.Pandas are shy; they don't venture into areas where people live.

Threats and Why They Matter

China’s Yangtze Basin region holds the panda’s primary habitat. Infrastructure development (such as dams, roads, and railways) is increasingly dividing and isolating panda populations, preventing pandas from finding new bamboo forests and potential mates.Forest loss also reduces pandas’ access to the bamboo they need to survive. The giant panda is an excellent example of an umbrella species conferring protection on many other species where they live. In other words, when we protect pandas, we invariably protect other animals that live around them, such as multicolored pheasants, the golden monkey, takin, and crested ibis.
